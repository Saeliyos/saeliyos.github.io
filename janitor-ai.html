🧠 Lorebooks Aren’t Magic: Let’s Get Real About Tokens and Context - Guide by Sael_000 on Janitor AI

Posted: June 22, 2025

There’s a lot of noise around lorebooks. So let’s clarify what they actually do—and what they don’t.

🔍 What Exactly Are Lorebooks?

Lorebooks are a manual structuring tool.

That’s it.

They’re a way for you—the developer—to organize background data (characters, timelines, world lore, whatever) in neat side panels that activate when triggered by certain words or patterns.

They save you the trouble of formatting and managing structure by hand. Think of them like prefab scaffolding for your context—you don’t have to build the whole damn thing yourself. They handle the structure for you.

But make no mistake:

  • The model still reads everything that gets triggered.
  • It still uses tokens.
  • And it still processes it with the same brain.

🧠 So Why Do People Think Lorebooks “Save Tokens”?

Because the UI is clean.

Because the structure is neat.

Because it feels like you have more space.

But here’s the truth:

  • Lorebooks don’t give you extra room in the model’s memory.
  • They don’t store data “somewhere else.”
  • And they’re not a second brain whispering in the model’s ear.

When a lorebook entry activates, it gets pulled into the context window—same as if you’d written it out manually. There’s no magic. No backdoor. Just visible structure management.

✅ So Are Lorebooks Bad?

Absolutely not.

They’re useful. Fantastic, even.

Especially for people juggling:

  • Complex universes
  • Recurring characters
  • Branching timelines
  • Lore that only needs to appear conditionally

Lorebooks help you stay organized. They make reuse easier. They eliminate clutter in your visible prompt and give you modular control over what shows up and when.

Just don’t confuse that with extra space. It’s all still tokens. You’re just choosing when to spend them.

Lorebooks are basically:
Template-driven structure modules that trigger content into the prompt when certain conditions are met.

But here’s the stripped-down truth:

  • They don’t expand memory.
  • They don’t compress tokens.
  • They don’t exist in a separate “storage zone.”

What they do is let you:

  • Pre-write modular context chunks.
  • Set conditions for when each piece activates.
  • Avoid hardcoding everything directly in the prompt.

Think of them like a dynamic index card system. Instead of giving the model all the cards at once, you let it pull the ones it needs—but every card it pulls still counts against your total token limit.

They’re smart scaffolding.
Not a cheat code.
Not a brain upgrade.

You’re still running on the same engine. Just keeping your toolkit tidy.

📌 The Bottom Line:

  • Lorebooks are not a second memory.
  • They don’t raise the context limit.
  • They don’t give your bot more space or brainpower.

They’re a developer-facing UI tool—one that helps you organize your structure without writing every detail out manually.

But once they fire, it’s business as usual for the model. Same input stream. Same token cap. Same rules.

🧵 TL;DR:

  • Lorebooks help you stay organized—not the model.
  • The bot doesn’t get more space—it just sees different parts at different times.
  • Clean structure still matters. Tokens are still tokens.

🧠 Restarting a Chat Because the Bot “Forgot Its Character”: This doesn't fix the problem, it resets it. - Guide by Sael_000 on Janitor AI

Posted: June 20, 2025

Restarting a chat is not the fix for a bot losing character in chat over time.

It resets the problem. It doesn’t fix it.

If a bot breaks character when the chat gets long, this is a design flaw in your bot.

Your bot shouldn’t go out of character when you hit memory context. Your bot should not need a reset button.

If it does, you’re relying too heavily on the welcome message or initial setup. That’s not sustainable.

Needing to transfer chat memories or start a new chat just to keep the bot in character means the bot was never truly taught how to stay in character.

You’re not fixing the problem—you’re just resetting it.

⚠️ What This Usually Means:

  • The character prompt is too shallow in the bot definition. It only exists in the welcome message or short-term memory.
  • When the memory limit is hit and older messages are dropped, the bot loses its reference point.
  • If the bot can’t maintain its tone, behavior, or perspective without constant reminders, then the core instruction isn’t strong enough.

✅ What to Do Instead:

  • Bake in the behavior, tone, and rules inside the personality and scenario fields. Don’t assume the bot will “remember” them—force it to carry those traits through every message.
  • Use clear, direct instruction inside the bot definition. The instructions must explicitly teach the AI that it is the character. For example:
    • “The AI’s role is {{char}}.”
    • “{{char}} embodies [name of character].”
    • “{{char}} always stays in character.”
  • Don’t waste memory slots on fixed character data. Those should be reserved for evolving plot/world events. Core traits should be hardcoded into the base definition.

Bottom line: If a bot breaks character when the chat gets long, this is a design flaw in the bot.

The character should be able to re-anchor itself without needing a fresh start. If your bot collapses after a long conversation, it wasn’t strong enough to begin with.

Build characters that stand without constant correction.

🧠 In-Depth Additional Explanation:

If your bots “forget their character on JLLM after like 100 messages,” this is not a token issue.

This is a structure and scripting issue in your character definition.

Whether your bot is high or low token doesn’t matter here.

  • If the bot is low token, it likely has an empty personality section or zero actual instructions—there’s literally nothing to read.
  • If the bot is high token but unreadable, it has poor structure and poor instructions—too vague, too messy, or misaligned with model parsing.

This issue is extremely common when bots are front-loaded with a welcome message that handles all the RP setup. When that message falls out of context, the character collapses.

If your bot forgets the roleplay or its character after a while, your writing structure isn’t being understood by the model.

Other creators will say it’s a token problem. They’re wrong.

This issue happens in both high and low token builds. It is not a token limit problem.

This is a writing problem.

Bad instructions = weak behavior anchoring. Doesn’t matter if you used 400 tokens or 4000.

Well-written bots can hold their identity across time. Poorly written ones can’t.

📌 TL;DR:

  • If your bot forgets its character, you’re not giving it a strong enough identity.
  • Restarting a chat resets the problem, it doesn’t solve it.
  • This isn’t JLLM’s fault. It’s a writing issue.
  • Proofread your structure. Strengthen your definitions.
